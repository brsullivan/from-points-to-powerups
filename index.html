Games are Universal

The first thing I think it's important to understand is that games are a universal experience. It would be impossible to know for sure how all 7 billion + people on the planet feel about games, but it's very likely that everyone enjoys a game in some form. It's possible to make this assumption because we have examples of games dating back over 5,000 years and spanning cultures across the world.

Games come in many shapes: video games, board games, card games, brain teasers like crossword puzzles - there's something for everyone and they all share some common ideas.

You wouldn't think Candy Land, Sudoku and Call of Duty have anything in common, but each has it's own set of rules with specific conditions for success, enough variability that the game won't be the same every time you play, and opportunities to modify the difficulty to fit the player's preferences.

This assumption is relevant to what we're talking about today because gamification essentially means taking your application and making it more like a game. Some people hear that and immediately think it is going to discourage some users from participating. I don't believe that's true. It all comes down to how you implement it.

What is Gamification?

Gamification is the process of applying game mechanics to any non-game activity.

Game Mechanics
Non-Game Activity
  • Two parts that are important here - game mechanics and what I mean when I say a non-game activity. We'll dive into the mechanics first.

Game Mechanics

    • Points
    • Leaderboards
    • Achivements
    • Levels
    • Competition & Challenges
    • Power-Ups

    Game mechanics are aspects of the rules or design that affects how a player experiences a game. This can include, but is not limited to, any of these items.

    Next, let's talk about the types of activities we might gamify.

    Spectrum of Activity

    • Gamification can be applied outside of tech solutions, but that's not what we're focusing on today.
    • Every digital interaction can be placed somewhere on a spectrum from game to non-game.
    • Where an application lands depends on the main goal of the implementation: Recreation - Education (safe to fail) - Motivation (real world consequences) - Function
    • Let's look at an example of gamification to understand how it works.

    Pain Squad

    This is an app called Pain Squad, which was created to replace the standard pain diaries children undergoing treatment for cancer are asked to complete.

    Pain history and management are essential pieces of treating childhood cancer so these records are very important, but it can be really difficult to get children to regularly fill out these logs. (Real World Consequences)

    A user is prompted to complete reports as if they were a detective investingating a case. They are rewarded for completing these investigations with badges, leveling up and messages delivered by popular TV police.(Worst case - a distraction, best case - motivation :: win-win)

    How Gamification Works

    • Intrinsic rewards for progress and performance
    • Creates feedback loop of achievement
    • Distraction from tedious tasks
    • Extrinsic rewards like more money and a better office only work to a point. One way gamification works is by leveraging intrinsic rewards like happiness and subjective well-being by promoting a sense of accomplishment.
    • When we achieve a goal, these positive feelings reinforce our positive behaviors - creating motivation to continue.
    • Another way gamification can help is by creating a distraction for tedious tasks like manual data entry. If you can make it entertaining, it won't lead to burnout which can actually improve overall quality.
    • A note: The goal of gamification is not addiction. You want to be really careful about encouraging or rewarding negative behaviors. This is where user experience design & testing can be really crucial for success.

    Other Examples

    • Foursquare
    • Duolingo
    • Foursquare used gamification to incentivize users to collect data - that would have otherwise cost them a tremendous amount of money to obtain.
    • Duolingo directs the user's motivation and engagement to produce faster learning.

    When should I Gamify?

    The application has:

    • Real-world outcomes & consequences
    • Tasks and processes that already exist
    • A need for engagement or motivation

    The defining characteristics that make an application a good candidate for gamification are:

    • It is not safe to fail. There are real-world consequences and measurable outcomes
    • The tasks or processes have to (or should) be done anyway. We're not creating tasks for the sake of gamifying them
    • Those completing the tasks would benefit from additional motivation or boosted engagement

    Technical Considerations

    Design, Develop & Test

    • Gamification is not a totally new paradigm. No matter your role, you can apply some of the standards and processes you already know well to deliver a great gamification product.
    • However, there are some specific things that make gamification unique that you should consider to create the best outcome.

    Designing Gamification

    1. Keep the game elements relevant to the task
    2. It doesn't have to be elaborate or difficult
    3. Account for the learning curve

    1:

    • You'll want to make sure you keep the context of your application in mind when deciding how to gamify.
    • Make sure you really understand your users because relevance to their interests can make or break your efforts.
    • Doesn't mean you can't have fun with it - just keep the context in mind. Quick tasks should have quick stories, save the longer plots for ongoing efforts. *** ADD MORE HERE ***

    2:

    • You can reap the rewards of gamification without developing an over-the-top, fully animated and silly experience. Particularly if you're gamifying in a corporate context, you don't want to make your users think you don't take their jobs seriously.

    3:

    • Gamifying an existing application doesn't mean you can skip the training. While you don't want to change the way a user performs the tasks, you can't expect them to immediately understand the rules.

    Developing Gamification

    The Good News: You can use a lot of the same development skills you already have to build gamification.

    The Bad News: Things like page load, performance speed and well-structured code become much more important.

    Testing Gamification

    • Higher reliance on manual testing
    • People are going to try and cheat the system
    • Beyond usability and functionality, you'll also be evaluating repetability and fatigue
