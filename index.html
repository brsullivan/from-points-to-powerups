From Points to Power-Ups:

How Gamification Can Level Up Your User Experience

Speaker

Braelyn Sullivan

Assumptions & Expectations

  • Games are universal
  • Games can look very different
  • Everyone plays games

Throughout this presentation, I will be making a couple of assumptions about games and the people who play them. By definition, these will always be assumptions because it would be impossible to figure out how each of the more than 7 billion people on the planet feel about games; however, I want to set up some expectations so that you'll understand why I feel comfortable making these assumptions.

First, I believe it's safe to assume games are universal because there are examples of games dating back over 5,000 year and spanning across the millenia. We also know games have been found in various cultures all across the world.

Second, I think it is important to understand that games can look very different, even while employing the same strategies and techniques. Sudoku, Clue and Call of Duty aren't likely to have many common characteristics, but each has a set of rules with specific conditions for success.

Finally, because of this wide variety of games and vast history of cultural significanc, I feel confident in assuming everyone enjoys at least one game.

Games come in many shapes & sizes:

  • Video Games (console, mobile, multiplayer, singleplayer, online, casual)
  • Card Games (solitaire, poker)
  • Group party games (Mafia)
  • Brain Teasers (sudoku, crossword, SET)
  • Board Games (Clue, Sorry)

What is Gamification?

Gamification is the process of applying game mechanics to any non-game activity.

  • Gamification can be applied outside of tech solutions
    • Chore charts to encourage kids to complete their tasks at home
    • Customer loyalty points/programs

Game Mechanics

Game Mechanics can include:

  • Points
  • Leaderboards
  • Achivements
  • Levels
  • Competition
  • Power-Ups
  • Challenges

Spectrum of Games

Spectrum of Games

Every digital interaction can be placed somewhere on a spectrum from game to non-game.

While this presentation will focus on gamification, serious games and gamification are both important areas for growth in business activities.

Recreation - Education (safe space to fail) - Motivation (real-world implication) - Function

Characteristics of Gamification

  • Real-world outcomes & consequences
  • Tasks and processes already exist
  • A need for engagement or motivation

The defining characteristics that separate gamification from the others are:

  • It is not safe to fail. There are real-world consequences and measurable outcomes
  • The tasks or processes have to (or should) be done anyway. We're not creating tasks for the sake of gamifying them
  • Those completing the tasks would benefit from additional motivation or boosted engagement

Let's look at an example of gamification to understand how it works.

Pain Squad

This is an app called Pain Squad. It's an app-based pain diary created for kids with cancer.

Pain history and management are essential pieces of treating childhood cancer so these records are very important, but it can be really difficult to get children to regularly fill out these logs.

A user is prompted to complete reports as if they were a detective investingating a case.

They are rewarded for completing these investigations with badges, leveling up and messages delivered by popular TV police.

Technical Considerations

Design, Develop & Test

  • Gamification is not a totally new paradigm. No matter your role, you can apply some of the standards and processes you already know well to deliver a great gamification product.
  • However, there are some specific things that make gamification unique that you should consider to create the best outcome.

Designing Gamification

  1. Keep the game elements relevant to the task
  2. Use complexity of tasks to guide the "fun factor"
  3. Account for the learning curve

1:

  • You'll want to make sure you keep the context of your application in mind when deciding how to gamify.
  • Make sure you really understand your users because relevance to their interests can make or break your efforts.
  • Doesn't mean you can't have fun with it - just keep the context in mind. Quick tasks should have quick stories, save the longer plots for ongoing efforts. *** ADD MORE HERE ***

2:

  • You can reap the rewards of gamification without developing an over-the-top, fully animated and silly experience. Particularly if you're gamifying in a corporate context, you don't want to make your users think you don't take their jobs seriously.

3:

  • Gamifying an existing application doesn't mean you can skip the training. While you don't want to change the way a user performs the tasks, you can expect them to immediately understand the rules.

Developing Gamification

The Good News: You can use a lot of the same development skills you already have to build gamification into a tool.

The Bad News: Things like page load, performance speed and well-structured code matter much, much more.
